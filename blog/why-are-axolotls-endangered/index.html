Axolotls live only in Lake Xochimilco in Mexico City. As the city grew into one of the largest in the world (over 21 million people), the lake shrank dramatically. Canals were drained, water was diverted, and the axolotl's habitat nearly disappeared.

In the 1970s, tilapia and Asian carp were introduced into the lake for fishing. These invasive fish eat axolotl eggs and compete for food. The axolotls had no way to defend themselves against these new predators.

Urban runoff, sewage, and agricultural chemicals have polluted the lake water. Axolotls breathe through their feathery gills, which makes them especially sensitive to poor water quality. Dirty water can make them sick or kill them.

Axolotls are incredibly important for science. Their ability to regenerate limbs, hearts, and even brain tissue is being studied by researchers worldwide. Understanding how axolotls heal could one day help humans recover from injuries and diseases. Losing them would mean losing a key to future medical breakthroughs.
